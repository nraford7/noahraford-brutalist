Biography

Esther O'Callaghan

Founder, Advisor & Social Impact Leader

OBE · Freeman of the City of London

Esther O'Callaghan is a founder, strategic advisor, and social impact leader whose career spans music, philanthropy, government, and technology. Recognized with an OBE for services to the voluntary sector and honored as a Freeman of the City of London, she has spent two decades building organizations, advising governments, and championing the next generation.

Her path defies convention. Growing up in a single-parent family on free school meals, she understood poverty firsthand—an experience that would shape everything that followed.

The Early Years: Manchester & Music

At 19, Esther moved to Manchester and became the first female DJ to own and run a specialist vinyl store in the UK. What began as a passion for music became a platform: she DJ'd across Europe, toured with Massive Attack, and joined the original production team for Gorillaz's "Demon Days Live" at Manchester International Festival.

But the record shop was never just about music. Using profits from events, she established a music foundation that helped 2,500 young people aged 14-16 re-engage with education, training, and employment—proving that creativity could be a vehicle for change.

If you want to engage young people in education, in learning, in skills development, you need to make it engaging, rewarding, and fun. You need to meet them where they are—and really decide if you want to raise humans to fit the system, or make the system fit around them.
— Esther O'Callaghan

Philanthropy & Public Service

Her grassroots work led to national recognition. Esther joined the Community Foundation for Greater Manchester, where she witnessed how even £500 could transform a local community group. This sparked a deeper commitment to philanthropy and systems change.

She went on to serve on the National Board of the Big Lottery Fund—the single largest lottery distributor for good causes across the UK—and the Spirit of 2012 Olympic Legacy Endowment Fund. At the government level, she contributed as an expert to the House of Lords skills white paper, shaping national policy on workforce development.

Founder: Building During the Storm

When COVID hit, Esther did what she'd always done: built something. Her startup supported a quarter million young people across the UK to develop the skills they needed for work. It was, by her own account, "the hardest piece of work I've ever done."

A first-time founder navigating a two-sided marketplace, she raised through the frothy angel and seed markets before the 2022 crash made everything harder. The company went through an acquisition of another business, and ultimately the startup itself was acquired—leaving Esther with hard-won lessons and deep empathy for what founders face.

"The benefit now is I can see the patterns of repeating mistakes that most founders make," she reflects. "Largely because they're too close to the problem they're trying to solve."

Today: Advisor & Advocate

Now based in the UAE, Esther serves on the advisory board of Global Tech Advocates, the world's largest volunteer-led network across the technology industry, where she has worked alongside founder Russ Shaw for seven years.

She mentors female-founded startups—two of her mentees independently went on to win Dragon's Den and are now building materially successful scale-ups. She remains a domain expert in skills, workforce policy, and the future of learning and work, with particular focus on emerging markets and intergenerational mobility.

A qualified British Triathlon coach, she finds her greatest joy in helping others succeed. "I get the most joy from seeing other people fulfill their goals," she says. "The joy comes from having contributed to that in a meaningful way."

You can't build the future without the future builders. A disenfranchised youth population at scale has, in history, generally not turned out that great.
— On the global skills gap
